Valorant doesn't need to be competitive (And every other game)

I've noticed for too long that video games are focusing more on E-Sports rather than making fun and innovative experiences. Now, don't get me wrong. Games like Valorant (Which is the focus of today's blog) are amazing and I have burnt hundreds of hours into this game. However, the company's focus on "competition" and "fair play", while fine in theory and great in moderation, tarnishes the fun of the game.

Don't get me wrong, I love me a hard game. I mean, I literally only play Minecraft on Hardcore, Sifu on the hardest difficulty, and Persona 5 on Hard. That being said, I do know the difference between a hard game, and a game you can't win.

Hard Vs. Impossible

I think that getting the difference between a hard game and a game that is simply unwinnable is definitely needed. While defining Hard vs. Impossible is Impossible Hard for online games due to the nature of the player base constantly changing, it isn't impossible. In short, matchmaking.
Matchmaking is the tool that tries to pair equally skilled players with each other, in the hopes of a fair fight. Some games do really well with it, like Tetris Effect Connected, despite most of the player base being better than me in every possible way. However, I have found that some games, like Valorant and Virtua Fighter 5 tend to push me towards a low Win/Lose rate. This ends up making some matches trivial, and others impossible, overall leading to minimal improvement. Valorant, especially, will do it's best to keep you at a 50% win rate, by alternating between easy matches and hard matches. This is not bad by any means, but it can demotivate players like myself.

I would like to re-iterate that I don't mean to say "I want a 100% win rate, and anything below that is bad.". I have lost tons of matches in Tetris and Gran Turismo 7[1], but there was always a learning opportunity for me in the matches I lost. Some games instead fluctuate between a trivial match and a match that is simply unwinnable, and every time I go for the unwinnable ones, I always come out wondering "What can I learn?" and realize that there was nothing to learn, because I was always killed before I had the opportunity to retaliate.

Setting up that balance is definitely important, and while you can have a good game without that balance (I still play Valorant even though I keep losing, it's just a fun game in general), that balance is the difference between a great game and a masterpiece. As a game developer, remember that the player, at the end of the day, is human. They do have a tolerance to losing, and winning too much will not encourage them to improve.

Player Base

Sadly, this is something that cannot be addressed by the developers of the game itself. This is totally dependent on the game's popularity and appeal, and inevitably, some games will suffer. That said, I do think that it is important to discuss.

Often times, the people playing the game are far more skilled than people just joining it. Some games I have noticed this to happen in are in Elite Dangerous and in Team Fortress 2, but that being said, they aren't bad communities in any way. Some people in Elite Dangerous' community are really friendly, but some are exceptionally rude. Just hope that you get the friendly ones and not the unfriendly ones.

If you are an experienced player, remember that you too were a newbie at one point, and if you are a newbie, don't take the rude comments to heart. I would've quit Valorant back in 2020 if I took everything of what a random dude on voice chat said to heart.

Fun

This mainly boils down to game design.

It's hard to make a fun game and a competitive game. It's not impossible, but it is really tough. Games like CSGO, Valorant and Smash Bros have mastered that art, with their simple to pick up game mechanics which can be used and abused in infinitely diverse ways. It's mostly the other glaring issues that affect how those mechanics are used, leading to "Metas". Metas are when a certain character or a certain build is used disproportionately more than others due to how powerful they are, or due to how easy it is to master that character.

Balancing is tough, and often times leads to mass outrage among the community. I haven't made any multiplayer games; I prefer to stick to single player games, but I will say, it is really tough to not only find that balance, but also to maintain it. Keep adding new characters and new items, eventually a meta will form, no matter how hard you suppress it.

Conclusion.

All in all, this is just a rant I wrote after losing in Valorant too many times. I do love the game, and I will keep playing it, and it's flaws are just nitpicks, but every game developer should strive to fix the base-line issues. I am no authority on what games should do, and how they should be, but still, it is important to consider all perspectives on the issue, even if it comes from an experienced game developer, or a newbie gamer. Games were made to be tools for enjoyment, and it's important to remember that, both as players, and developers.
