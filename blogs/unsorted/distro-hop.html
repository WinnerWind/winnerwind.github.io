What distro is good for me?

I've heard a lot of people who switch to linux ask themselves "What distro is good for me?", receive a thousand responses, all giving their own distro, and eventually, the person getting confused and gives up entirely.

While yes, this article by no means a perfect means to the end. This is probably going to be super flawed, and your opinion will differ, and that's completely fine. In a way, that's what makes Linux so great. This guide is meant to enable you to come to the conclusion that yes, this distro is right for me.

The LiveCD

Before we get started, it's worth mentioning the LiveCD.

Most Linux distros come with a LiveCD mode, where you can use the entire operating system without actually installing it. The entire system will run a bit slower than if it was fully installed on the system, but what matters is that it will run and will be fully functional. In fact, you install the operating system from the LiveCD mode.

After flashing your USB with your operating system of choice, you will be able to boot into it. After this, you're in the LiveCD mode.

In the below discussion, every distro which has a fully functional LiveCD will be marked with a icon

Introduction

People seem to have the wrong idea about the purpose of a Linux Distro. Each distro has their own specific use case. While yes, you can use each distro for purposes outside of it's designed purpose, in most cases it's more of a headache than actually using a distro for your use case.

Also, I want you to consider why you use your computer. I've seen some people who ask this question end up saying that "Oh, I just browse the web" or "I just watch YouTube on my laptop" and to that I say Like smartphones, every Linux distro can do that just fine

Unless you really do something unconventional, like for example, your job demands that you use Photoshop, or you really like Valorant, switching to Linux might be a bit of a daunting task. That said, you can have the best of both worlds by dual-booting, something I won't get into right now.

What do I use?

Top Pick : Linux Mint

Learn More Linux Mint Website
Let's just cut to the chase. If you just need a system, and just need it to browse videos or need it to be your daily driver, Linux Mint is by far the most flexible option. You don't want to use a brain and decide what distro is good for you? Just go for mint, no questions asked. You'll feel very much at home.

It's well supported, has a loving community and also works out of the box without much configuration. I think that it's interface is quite visually pleasing, especially if you're coming over from Windows. It's super fast and lightweight, and has three different variants based on whether you prefer visual fidelity or need something blazing fast. When I was using mint, I barely had to touch the terminal other than a few annoying errors that couldn't be fixed via traditional GUI tools.

Runners Up : Ubuntu

Learn More Ubuntu Website
Ubuntu is similar to Linux Mint in several ways. It's almost identical. Ubuntu does have a loving community behind it, but it does have some level of instability. Ubuntu, being larger, does have more support, but it's little tiny bugs are quite annoying. All in all, definitely a solid choice if you wish to try out Linux, and it was my first Linux distribution too. It served me well for over a year.

Ubuntu is a bit stuttery and a tad bit heavy on the system, but it isn't the end of the world. It's got a large amount of packages and support, and beats Linux Mint just by the sheer amount of people working on it at the same time. You can probably use the system for months without ever touching the terminal.

Powerful Yet Accessible : Manjaro

Learn More Manjaro Website
Manjaro is another daily driver operating system based on Arch Linux. It's certainly not as well supported as Ubuntu or Mint, but it's still got a decent amount of support and love. It's quite well maintained and focuses on accessibility and user-friendliness over anything else. You will need to dabble with the terminal a bit, but that's a good thing, because that helps train you for other, more powerful distros.

It does come with KDE Plasma, a desktop which is known to be quite heavy on systems, however, you can always change that later down the line.

For the Pretty Peacock : Arch Linux

Learn More Arch Linux Website
Definitely not your first distro. If you have decent grip on the Linux terminal, you might like Arch. It's super configurable and very lightweight. My boot time on a 2017 Lenovo X1 Carbon is just 3 seconds. It's fit for daily use, like I have been doing for over six months. You like to take control? Get Arch.

Very light on storage too! My entire set-up fits in 64GB, and I do play retro games on Steam Proton from time to time. The installer ISO is only 1GB big. It's super lightweight and customisable to death. It supports almost every major Desktop environment, including fancy ones like Hyprland. Here's my setup!

A Flexible Setup

Now, onto the real reason I wrote this blog in the first place. A flexible setup is essential if you have not decided on which distro you want to go for. I mean, it's fair, and its fine that you don't know what distro is good for you[1]. You can even make a setup that preserves all your files even if you switch distros.

Reasoning

There are multiple ways to make a flexible setup, but in this one, the only thing we will preserve when switching distros is your home folder. This has a few advantages.

  • Advantages:
    • Trivial to do
    • Natively supported
    • Preserve important documents and files with ease.
    • Can preserve your configuration files
  • Disadvantages:
    • Need to re-install all your programs
    • Less flexible than using a single partition setup
    • Need to calculate disk spaces in advance.

Setting up a system for flexibility.

We'll start by assuming that you're starting fresh and that you are not dual booting. If you are dual booting, then it shouldn't be too hard to change this setup to your needs.

Step 0: Setup

Things you'll need: A USB with at least 8GB, we'll be deleting all files from it.

Follow the Balena Etcher guide in the Arch Linux Guide but instead install Ubuntu. We're not installing Ubuntu to your system, we're taking advantage of the fact that it comes with GParted, a convenient partition editor.

Step 1: Partitioning your system.

We'll be splitting your home folder using GParted partition editor. There's tons of tutorials online, and remember to not accidentally format used partitions. At the end of it, you should have a partition structure that is like this. Make sure that your new partitions (the root and home partitions) are formatted as ext4.

  • Your boot partition (for UEFI systems)
  • A root partition (at least 10GB and preferably not more than 30GB)
  • A home partition (As much space as you need)
  • Swap Partition (If any)
  • Backups Partition (if any)
  • Your Windows partitions (if you are dualbooting)

Step 2: Pointing Linux at it.

Most GUI based installers like that of Mint and Ubuntu provide a Custom Install option. Remember to tell Mint that the tiny root partition goes for / and the larger home partition goes for /home/. If you are installing Arch Linux, the commands are these.

mount /dev/root_partition /mnt
mount /dev/home_partition /mnt/home

Proceed installing as normal.

Distro-hopping.

Now that you have a flexible setup. You can "hop" between distros by simply formatting the root partition while telling the new, to be installed, Linux Distro that the home partition is simply a mount-point.

Enjoy!

Conclusion

I would like to state that this isn't a "end all be all" guide for you. You are not obligated to use my suggestion. But, it is my opinion that if you are just starting out Linux Mint or Ubuntu are fantastic choices. If you feel like jumping straight into the deep end, Arch Linux is a fine choice. In fact, Arch was my first distro! I installed it and made a Arch Linux Installation Guide!
I wish you luck on your Linux journey!
